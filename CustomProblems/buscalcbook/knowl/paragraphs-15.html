Every \(f\) has multiple antiderivatives.

Consider the function \(f(x)=x^2\text{.}\) There are many functions whose derivatives are \(x^2\text{:}\)

\begin{align*} \ddxfrac{\frac{x^3}{3} } \amp = \frac{1}{3}3\cdot x^2 \amp = x^2 \\ \ddxfrac{\frac{x^3}{3} + 5 } \amp = \frac{1}{3}3\cdot x^2 + 0 \amp = x^2 \\ \ddxfrac{\frac{x^3}{3} - 2 } \amp = \frac{1}{3}3\cdot x^2 + 0 \amp = x^2 \end{align*}

Following this pattern, notice that if \(F(x)\) is one antiderivative of f(x), then \(F(x)+C \) is also an antiderivative.
