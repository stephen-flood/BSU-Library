To understand the meaning of \(\int\) notation, let's think of the meaning of cumulative and marginal cost.

We have already seen that if

  • marginal cost = \(C'(x) \approx\) item cost and

  • total cost = \(C(x)\text{,}\) then

  • \(\intdx{C'(x)} = C(x) + a\) for some constant \(a\text{.}\)

    Note: we have switched notation here to avoid confusion. In this case, since \(C(x)\) refers to a function, we use a lower case \(a\) to denote our unknown constant.

Suppose you want to find the cost of items 10 through 100 only. There are two ways to find this

  1. By adding up up the item costs of items 10 to 100.

    Because \(C'\) approximates the cost of the next item, this is approximated by computing

    \begin{equation*} C'(9)+C'(10)+C'(11) + \dots + C'(99) \end{equation*}

  2. By subtracting finding the total cost of items 1-9 from the total cost of items 1-100.

    \begin{equation*} C(100) - C(9) \end{equation*}

Condensing this notation, we get

\begin{equation*} \stackrel{ \scriptstyle \text{to 99} }{ \stackrel{\textstyle\text{Sum}}{ \scriptstyle \text{from 9} } } C'(x) \approx C(100) - C(9) \end{equation*}

The art of approximating well with calculus is the subject of several courses (including analysis and numerical analysis).

For our purposes here, it is enough to say that 99 and 100 are "approximately equal," as are 9 and 10, so we can really say

\begin{equation*} \stackrel{ \scriptstyle\text{to 100} }{ \stackrel{\textstyle\text{Sum}}{ \scriptstyle\text{from 10} } } C'(x) \approx C(100) - C(10) \end{equation*}

If you further simplify the word "Sum" to just the first letter \(S\text{,}\) you get

\begin{equation*} \stackrel{ \scriptstyle\text{to 100} }{ \stackrel{\textstyle\text{S}}{ \scriptstyle\text{from 10} } } C'(x) \approx C(100) - C(10) \end{equation*}

which looks remarkably similar to

\begin{equation*} \intdx{_{10}^{100} \, C'(x)} = C(100) - C(10) \end{equation*}

This similarity is not accidental. In fact, this is the entire reason we use the symbol \(\int\) for indefinite integrals and antiderivatives.
