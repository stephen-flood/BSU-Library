First, we must find the critical points. In other words, find the \(x\) such that \(f'(x) =0\) or \(f'(x) \ DNE\text{.}\)

First, find an equation for the derivative \(f'\text{,}\) and factor it.

\begin{align*} f'(x) = \amp \ddx{ \frac{-1}{4} x^4 + \frac{8}{3} x^3 + 4 } \\ = \amp \frac{-1}{4}\cdot 4x^3 + \frac{8}{3} \cdot 3x^2 + 0 \\ = \amp -x^3 + 8x^2 \end{align*}

To find when \(f'=0\text{,}\) simply solve the equation

\begin{equation*} -x^3 + 8x^2 = 0 \end{equation*}

Solve the equation by factoring. It is easier to factor equations when the highest power term has a factor of 1. To do this, factor out a \(-x^2\) from both terms

\begin{align*} (-x^2) \cdot (x - 8) \amp = 0 \end{align*}

The only way this can happen is if either the first or second terms equal 0. In other words, solve

\begin{align*} x = 0 \amp \text{ or } \amp x-8=0 \\ x = 0 \amp \text{ or } \amp x=8 \end{align*}

We have a singularity when \(f'\ DNE\text{.}\) Looking at the graph of \(f\text{,}\) it does not look like there are any singularities.

Looking at the function, the derivative \(f'(x) = -x^2(x-8)\) is always defined. This confirms that this function \(f\) does not have any singularities.
